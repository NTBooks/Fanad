Why a separate room

The house renovation has forty tasks, and they're drowning out “call the pharmacy.” Or you want work stuff somewhere your Sunday-morning whatdo will never surface it. Or you're organizing something bounded — a wedding, a move, an estate — that deserves its own clean pad and a door you can close.

Notebooks are exactly that: like a fresh account you can walk into and back out of.

Where am I?

The one habit notebooks ask of you: know which room you're standing in. If you're ever unsure:

notebook

📓 work📖 Back to main

notebook main (or just home) takes you back. Notebooks never nest, and switching is instant — it's normal to pop into work for one capture and pop back out.

What carries across, what doesn't

  • Separate per notebook: tasks, notes, lists — the content.
  • Shared across all your spaces: your module choices, your mood, and your chat itself. Reminders still find you in the same conversation regardless of where they were set.
  • Private: a notebook is yours alone. Nobody else — vouched-in friends included — can reach into it.

Good notebook shapes

  • A bounded project: the renovation, the move, the conference you're running. When it ends, the clutter ends with it — your main pad never absorbed any of it.
  • A role: work, kept strictly apart so weekend suggestions stay weekend-shaped.
  • A person you're helping: managing a parent's appointments and paperwork in its own space keeps their affairs coherent and out of your personal list.
  • A someday-shelf: a someday notebook for the ideas you refuse to delete but don't want ranked against real life.
Does it deserve a notebook?

The test is simple: do you want these tasks suggested to you alongside everything else? If yes, a category is enough. If the honest answer is “only when I've deliberately sat down for it” — that's a notebook.
